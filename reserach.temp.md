## Cross-Cutting Patterns: What Makes These Descriptions Work for Search Committees

### Pattern 1: Problem-First, Not Tool-First
All four lead with the **problem** they solve or the **goal** they pursue, not with the techniques they use. Meng Xu leads with "finding and patching vulnerabilities in critical systems"; Alex Xu leads with "dependable infrastructure for everyday life"; Wang leads with "software reliability and performance"; Naik leads with "reliable and trustworthy" AI reasoning. Techniques (fuzzing, static analysis, neurosymbolic methods) come second. **Search committees hire people who solve important problems, not people who are good at a particular hammer.**

### Pattern 2: A Memorable Intellectual Thesis or Brand
Each person stakes out a distinctive position that goes beyond topic keywords:
- Xu (Waterloo): "security as a first-class citizen"
- Xu (Purdue): "intelligence engineering"
- Wang: neuro-symbolic static analysis intersection
- Naik: "programs as structured graphs, not text"

These function as **slogans** -- they are what a committee member repeats when advocating for a candidate in a faculty meeting. Your current bio lacks this -- it reads as a topic list, not an intellectual position.

### Pattern 3: Structured Research Program (Not a Paper List)
Three of the four explicitly organize their work into 2-3 named research pillars or driving questions. This signals that the candidate has a **program**, not just a collection of papers. It answers the implicit committee question: "What will this person's lab look like in five years?"

### Pattern 4: Classical Roots, Modern Frontier
Every one anchors in an established discipline (systems security, software engineering, static analysis, program synthesis) and extends it into a timely frontier (Rust/Move, agentic AI, LLMs for code, neurosymbolic AI). This dual grounding is strategically effective: it **reassures senior committee members** who value fundamentals while **exciting junior members and administrators** who want forward-looking hires.

### Pattern 5: Conciseness and Scanability
None exceed a few short paragraphs. Faculty search committees review hundreds of applications; the research description must communicate its core in 30-60 seconds. All four achieve this.

### Pattern 6: Societal or Practical Impact Framing
Each connects technical work to a larger outcome a non-specialist can appreciate: secure critical infrastructure, dependable AI for everyday life, reliable software, trustworthy AI-generated code. This converts a "solid technical researcher" into a "strategic hire."
